Choosing Your Keyboard Layout

The layout of your keyboard determines its size and functionality. Here are some popular options:

  • Full-Size: Includes a numpad, function row, and navigation cluster.
  • Tenkeyless (TKL): Removes the numpad for a more compact design.
  • 75%, 65%, and 60%: Smaller layouts for minimalists or gamers who need more desk space. Know that they still have all the same functionality as the bigger ones, they just require an extra button press to get to some of the buttons.
  • Split Keyboards: Ergonomic designs that reduce wrist strain and allow tenting.
  • Alternative Layouts: Options like Dvorak or Colemak can improve typing efficiency.

Ensure your keycap set matches the layout of your board, especially for non-standard or compact layouts.

Key Switches

Switches determine how your keyboard feels and sounds. Here are the main types:

  • Linear: Smooth and quiet, great for shared spaces like school, a coffee shop, or dorm room.
  • Tactile: Provide a bump for feedback, ideal for typing and more satisfying to press in my opinion. This is what most pre-built keyboards use.
  • Clicky: Loud and satisfying to hear, but will annoy anyone around you.

Consider the actuation force (measured in grams) that suits your needs. Heavier switches reduce accidental key presses, while lighter switches can minimize fatigue during long typing sessions.

If you are new I would recommend purchasing a switch sample pack to try out different options before deciding on your favorite.

Stabilizers

Stabilizers keep larger keys like the spacebar and Enter stable and smooth. I would recommended to purchase aftermarket stabilizers for better quality. You are going to need to lube them generously for optimal performance. I would also consider the band-aid mod to reduce noise (explained in the build guide; all you need is a couple band-aids).

PCB and Case

Your PCB is the brain of the keyboard, and your case provides structure and sound characteristics.

  • Hot-Swappable PCBs: Ideal for beginners and those who like to experiment with switches.
  • Soldered PCBs: More stable but require soldering skills.
  • Case Materials: Aluminum for rigidity, polycarbonate for a softer feel, and brass for a louder sound.

Keycaps

Keycaps influence the feel and aesthetics of your keyboard. Here are the main types:

  • ABS: Smooth and shiny but may wear over time.
  • PBT: Durable with a matte texture, resists wear.
  • Double-Shot: Legends never fade as they are molded into the plastic.
  • Dye-Sublimated: High-quality legends embedded into PBT keycaps.

Some tips to think about when purchasing keycaps is that white ones don't look as worn as colored or black ones will over time.

Also make sure that you also take into account the size of the keycaps. Cherry is the standard size, but the bigger they are, the deeper the sound they will make.

Extra Components

While building a custom keyboard, you may find these tools and accessories helpful:

The Board Parts

  • novelkeys (This is a good site for boards and switches, but it can be expensive and shipping can take awhile)
  • Drop (This one is pretty good if you are looking for something already put together or licsnesed like Marvel, Starwars, or Lord of the Rings. but it is also expensive)
  • Amazon (We all know and love amazon, it is great in the keyboard world if you are looking for something on the cheaper end. You can even get a pre built hot swap from them and switch out parts when you have a little extra money which makes it the best one for a budget build)
  • Etsy (If you are looking for a custom split board I would recommend etsy, it is going to be the most expensive, but it allows for the most customizable options. It can be daunting though so make sure you know what you want before you make a purchase)
  • Keychron (This one is a little newer so they are still getting their feet on the ground. But they have a wide variety of prebuilt hot swap boards. You could easily get one of these and not even modify it. But they are hotswappable and look really sleek so they are popular in the community)

Necessities

  • Krytox 205g0 Lube (Get this no matter what. There is other lube out there but this is the best by far)
  • Tweezers
  • Small paint brush
  • Small hobby-sized screwdriver
  • Switch puller (usually included in switch purchases)
  • Keycap puller (usually included in keycap purchases)

Strongly Recommended

  • Switch opener (This will save at least 60 minutes)
  • Wide blue painter's tape (This will improve the overall sound quality)
  • Foam inserts (This will dampen the sound and also make the board sound deeper)
  • Mini electric screwdriver (in place of hobby screwdriver)
  • Switch tray(This will improve your overall experience. I included a link for one, but pick your own. This will effect your overall experience, but if you can't afford one feel free to use cereal bowls or whatever kind of containers you have lying around)

Non-Useful Tools

Some tools may not provide significant value and can be skipped unless you have a specific reason to use them:

  • O-Rings: These are used for dampening sound but are unnecessary if you are already lubing your switches.
  • Switch Films: They are time-consuming to install for almost no benefit. I have always regretted installing them in my builds.
